ANGUL

1.The Budhi Thakurani temple is a prominent religious site in Angul, Odisha, located on the Sunasagad hill. The main deity, Maa Budhi Thakurani, is represented by a black granite stone pillar. The temple is not only a religious center but also a tourist attraction, drawing thousands of visitors annually due to its beautiful natural surroundings and dense forests. Apart from Budhi Thakurani, the temple complex also houses shrines dedicated to various deities such as Lord Ganesh, Sri Ram Laxman, Sita, Radha Krishna, Shani Devi, Lord Shiva, and Nava Graha. Additionally, there is a Lord Jagannath Temple known as Saila Srikhetra situated behind the Budhi Temple. The origin of the worship of Budhi Thakurani in Angul is not precisely documented, but there is a local belief that the area was ruled by a Kandha named Sunas, who used the hill as his Gada (mace).

2. The Deerjang Reservoir Project, located 7 km from Angul, is a significant medium irrigation project and water reservoir primarily designed for irrigation purposes. Construction began in 1960 and was completed during 1977-78. The dam is a zoned fill dam, spanning 1875m in length and reaching a maximum height of 27.31m, with a top width of 4.57m. Positioned across the Lingara and Mathili rivers near Majhika village in Angul district, the dam intercepts a catchment area of 399 sq. km. The Derjang Dam is an attractive destination for group picnics and relaxation. It offers a serene setting for enjoying sunrise or sunset, with the charming beauty of the green hills and forests surrounding the reservoir. Majhika village, near the dam, is an ideal spot to spend quality time with friends and family. The Derjang Irrigation Project is a reservoir scheme constructed across the Lingara and Matalia rivers near Majhika village in Angul Block.

3. Deulajhari, an ancient Saivite citadel in the Angul district of Odisha, is situated 6 km from Athamallik and 90 km from Angul. Nestled in a dense indigenous Jasmine forest, the temple is surrounded by high walls formed by the forest. Notably, the site is renowned for its hot springs, originally numbering eighty-four but now reduced to twenty-four, such as Agnikunda, Taptakunda, Himakunda, Annantakunda, and Labakusa Kunda. The water temperature in these springs ranges from 40 to 62 degrees Celsius. Covering an expansive 24 acres, the temple complex houses the main temple dedicated to the presiding deity Sidheswara Baba. The temple is a significant historical and religious site, with its ancient roots in Shaivism. The landscape is adorned with indigenous pandanus forest, locally known as kiabana. The unique combination of an ancient Shiva temple, a lush forest setting, and the therapeutic hot springs makes Deulajhari a captivating and culturally rich destination in Odisha.

4. Malayagiri, located near Pal Lahara town in Odisha, is a stunning mountain peak in the Malayagiri hills subrange of the Garhjat Range. Though not the tallest in Odisha, standing at 1,187 meters, it is the third-highest peak in the state. The mountain's allure lies in its natural beauty, featuring lush forests that attract visitors seeking a picturesque hill station experience. Known locally as Malyagiri, this mountain is a source of pride for the Angul district. The landscape is adorned with misty clouds, and a refreshing cold breeze makes it an ideal destination for trekking, offering unforgettable moments. The sunrise and sunset views are particularly enchanting, providing a captivating atmosphere. Malayagiri offers panoramic vistas of nearby hills, creating a serene and charming retreat for nature enthusiasts and those seeking the tranquility of a mountainous landscape in Odisha.

5. Nalco is the Major company Indian Aluminium Industry. Its around 12 km distance from Angul town. Nalco is self-sufficient in aluminum and gives the country a hi-tech edge in producing this metal to the best of world standards. Nalco was incorporated in 1981 in the Public Sector to exploit a part of the large deposits of bauxite discovered on the East Coast. There is also a beautiful park inside the Nalco Nagar.

6. Minna Jhola lies in the confluence of three rivers and offers the right ambience to relax and rejuvenate. Minna Jhola has a famous Shiva Temple which makes the town busy during the holy Shiv Ratri. The festival is celebrated with huge pomp and fervour. Local women thrive to this temple to pay their offerings.Thick and dense forest has made its dwelling on the bank of the river. The town itself is believed to be holy because according to Hindu mythology any confluence in India is considered to be very sacred. Minna Jhola can easily be reached by hiring a taxi or an auto. Bus is the cheapest mode of transportation which is also readily available in this place.

7. Rengali Dam, located in Odisha, is a multipurpose project situated 92 km from Angul along the Brahmani River. The dam serves the purposes of power generation, flood control, and irrigation. Standing at 70.5 meters tall and spanning 1040 meters in width, the Rengali Dam reservoir is the second-largest in Odisha, covering 37,840 hectares at full level and 28,000 hectares at mean level. The reservoir has a catchment area of 25,250 km2, primarily consisting of forests and wasteland. The dam, equipped with five units generating 50MW each, plays a crucial role in electricity production. With a capacity to hold 3412 million cubic meters of water at Full Reservoir Level, the dam significantly contributes to flood control. Downstream, a barrage is constructed across the Brahmani River, situated 35 km away from Rengali Dam.

8. Saila Srikhetra, also known as the Jagannath Temple in Angul, Odisha, is a significant religious site situated atop the Sunasagad hill. The temple's construction, modeled after the world-famous Lord Jagannath Temple in Puri, commenced in 1996 and was completed in 2002. The site is now referred to as Sailashreekhetra and attracts visitors not only from Angul but also from across Odisha and other parts of India. The temple complex is dedicated to Lord Jagannath, Lord Balabhadra, and Devi Subhadra and is considered one of the four sacred places in Odisha. It boasts a unique fusion of Kalinga and Dravidian architectural styles, adorned with intricate carvings and sculptures. The annual Rath Yatra is a major festival celebrated with enthusiasm, drawing devotees from far and wide. According to local beliefs, Lord Jagannath established the Saila Srikhetra temple complex during his exile, having a vision of Lord Shiva on the Saila hills in the Dwapara Yuga.

9. Satkosia Tiger Reserve is a significant wildlife sanctuary situated at the confluence of the Deccan Peninsula and the Eastern Ghats in Odisha, India. The reserve spans an extensive area of 988.30 kmÂ², encompassing the Satkosia Gorge Wildlife Sanctuary and the Baisipalli Wildlife Sanctuary. Established in 1976, the sanctuary features a 22 km long gorge through which the Mahanadi River flows. The diverse fauna in the reserve includes mammals like leopards, Indian wild dogs, wild boars, striped hyenas, sloth bears, and various deer species. The Asian elephant is abundant, often seen crossing rivers, alongside langurs, porcupines, pangolins, and the Indian giant squirrel. Reptiles such as the Mugger Crocodile and gharials inhabit the area, while the Indian python preys on larger mammals like spotted deer.

10. Sureswari Waterfall, nestled within the Bampur forest range in Angul, is located 40 km from Bampur Chowk on the way to Cuttack. Surrounded by dense and thick forests, the waterfall is a picturesque destination attracting thousands of visitors. Its scenic beauty is particularly stunning during the winter, making it an ideal spot for picnics. Despite being relatively lesser-known, Sureswari Waterfall was recently discovered by adventurer Subhasish Dash with the help of locals. Situated in the Bamur forest range, which falls under the Rairakhol sub-division on the Angul-Sambalpur border, the waterfall is accessible by traveling 40 km from Bamur Chowk towards Cuttack-Sambalpur highway, covering 30 km by motorbike and the remaining distance by trekking. Subhasish Dash shared his experience, mentioning that the journey involved passing through the last village of Angul, named Pathargarh, and traversing mountains and streams. Despite facing challenges, the allure of exploring a lesser-known waterfall kept them motivated.
